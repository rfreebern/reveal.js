The UX of URLs

Ryan Freebern / @rfreebern
  • UX begins before user has reached your site.
  • Explain how we know this, what it means, what makes up a URL, what makes a URL good, and why it matters.

What is a URL?

RFC 1738

“This document describes the syntax and semantics for a compact string representation for a resource available via the Internet. These strings are called ‘Uniform Resource Locators’ (URLs).”

Tim Berners-Lee et al, 1994
  • The web is the single biggest collaborative knowledge-sharing effort ever undertaken by the human race.
  • Globe-spanning ad-hoc machine.
  • We need to interact with it to make it useful.

HTTP Spoken Here

“We are building clans around languages we speak to the Machine.”

Vyacheslav Egorov, 2013
  • URLs are nouns in HTTP.
  • Platonic ideal URL: short, semantic, well-defined, unchanging, refers to a specific piece of information.
  • Programming languages are for physical machines.
  • Physical machines talk to each other and humans through HTTP.
  • Without URLs, communication breaks down.

Why Design URLs?

  1. Because people care.
  2. Because machines care.
  3. Because we can.

People Care

  • Consciously and unconsciously.
  • Depends on who they are and how they’re using the URLs.
  • They care about different things depending on their needs.
  • Developer/API, college student/research paper URL, marketer/advertisement/mom.

People Care

“[P]eople spent 25% of their time looking at the URL in navigational tasks vs. 22% in informational tasks.”

Edward Cutrell & Zhiwei Guan, 2007

  • Microsoft Research, eyetracking study, search engine behavior.
  • 24% of time on average looking at URLs.
  • "People just search and click on what looks best!"

Machines Care

  • Machines have needs and expectations about URLs.
  • Google's machines can affect your personal and business goals.

Because We Can

  • No matter what part we play, we care.
  • We want every part to be thoughtfully, respectfully crafted.
  • Don't just use any old URL.

What Makes a URL Good?

    Known knowns
    Planned for from the start
    Known unknowns
    Auto-generated from user interaction
    Unknown unknowns
    Added in the future, after you get hit by a truck
  • Good URL = sum of good parts. Every piece of the puzzle.
  • General plan won't cut it. Cover all the bases.
  • Known knowns: plan for initially. About contact page, major sections and subsections, user profiles, etc.
  • Known unknowns: from user interaction. Know they'll be there, don't know what they'll contain.
  • Blog post? 300 new products? New home on the market?
  • Unknown unknowns: Added by other developers after you're gone. We all get hit by a truck. Plan ahead.
  • We'll get to plan later. First, what makes the parts good?

  • Up to question mark: UTF-8. After: document's encoding.
  • Except if document is UTF-16; then UTF-8.
  • Recently saw a quote: "When in doubt, use UTF-8. When not in doubt, you're probably wrong and should still use UTF-8."
  • Special characters? Percent-encoding, but the special characters for the path are different from the query.

“%2B or not %2B?”

  • Allowed unescaped in path segment: :@-._~!$&'()*+,;=
  • Allowed unescaped in query parameter: /?:@-._~!$'()* ,;
  • Allowed unescaped in query value: /?:@-._~!$'()* ,;=
  • Allowed unescaped in fragment: /?:@-._~!$&'()*+,;=
  • Valid URL: http://example.com/:@-._~!$&'()*+,=?/?:@-._~!$'()*+,;=/?:@-._~!$'()*+,;==#/?:@-._~!$&'()*+,;=
  • URL spec: http://url.spec.whatwg.org
  • In path, plus is fine unescaped, spaces become %20.
  • In query, plus is %2B, spaces can be %20 or literal +.
  • It's essentially impossible to remember what characters are allowed where.
  • Anne van Kesteren is working on URL spec to improve this.

Slugs

  • Slugs are short, keyword-rich summaries.
  • Don't add to hackability, but benefit readability, credibility, and searchability.
  • Implementation should allow hackability.

Slugs

Canonical URL: http://example.com/documents/12345/title-of-the-document

Requested URL: http://example.com/documents/12345

Response: 

                            HTTP/1.1 301 Moved Permanently

Slug Rules

  1. Stick to alphanumerics and hyphen
  2. Get rid of common words (conjunctions, articles, etc.)
  3. Include something unique (an ID or a date)
  4. (or store the slug with the document)
  • Don't reinvent the wheel.
  • Literally dozens of slug libraries on packagist.

Good URLs are

Shareable

  • Let's talk about sharing.
  • Sharing is everywhere
  • Already talked about short, sensible, and snappy.

Robustness

  • http://example.com/2013/08/1298654-robust-urls-are-best
  • http://example.com/2013/08/1298654 → 301 Moved Permanently
  • http://example.com/2013/08/1298654-robust-urls-a → 301 Moved Permanently
  • http://example.com/2013/08/1298654-ha-ha-you-suck → 404 Not Found
  • Robustness: hard to screw up when shared.
  • Dropping the slug shouldn’t result in a 404.
  • Dropping a portion of the slug shouldn’t result in a 404, as long as there’s enough there to identify what’s being requested.
  • Why? Copy-and-paste errors that truncate your URL shouldn’t penalize the user. Platforms that erroneously insert linebreaks shouldn’t penalize the user.
  • Changing the slug to something else entirely, however, should.
  • Additionally, avoid generating URLs that contain encoded characters, because the way they’re shared could result in aka double-encoding.

Shortness

http://example.com/article/august-2013/good-url-design

bit.ly!

  • You have a beautiful URL. Now you want to shorten it.
  • Use bit.ly?
  • Short, snappy URL, quick to type.
  • Results in nicely-crafted full URL.
  • Provides all sorts of metrics.
  • Win-win, right?

No.

  • Third-party URL shorteners are bad for the web and bad for usability.
  • To quote Jason Scott:

“URL shorteners may be one of the worst ideas, one of the most backward ideas, to come out of the last five years. ... [T]hese general-purpose URL shorteners, with their shady or fragile setups and utter dependence upon them, well. If we lose TinyURL or bit.ly, millions of weblogs, essays, and non-archived tweets lose their meaning. Instantly. To someone in the future, it’ll be like everyone from a certain era of history […] started speaking in a one-time pad of cryptographic pass phrases.”

Jason Scott, 2011
  • Add a layer of fragility
  • Outside of your control
  • Zero accountability, no contracts, no guarantees.
  • Handing off marketing strategy
  • Eliminate credibility, robustness, searchability, everything you worked for.
  • You can tell I'm a big fan.

I Want Short URLs Anyway

  • Host your own: YOURLS (and other packages).
  • <link rel=”shorturl” href=”...”>
  • Bonus points:
    • Douglas Crockford's Base32 algorithm
    • Ryan Freebern's Easier encoder
  • Undeniably useful, or wouldn't be so prevalent.
  • Keep benefits without exposing yourself to drawbacks.
  • Build convenience into your platform.
  • Allows hand-crafting short URLs when appropriate.
  • Associates short domain with your brand == improves credibility.
  • Base32/Easier sacrifice shortness to improve robustness.

Canonical

  • One URL for one document
  • 301 any alternates
  • Rewrite trailing slashes

URLs are forever

“When someone follows a link and it breaks, they generally lose confidence in the owner of the server. They also are frustrated—emotionally and practically from accomplishing their goal.”

Tim Berners-Lee, 1998
  • Robustness includes persistence.
  • Do everything you can to keep old URLs working.
  • Another reason to plan a solid URL design from the start.
  • Real world: stuff moves. Company bought, platform change, rebuilt by third-party.

Know Your Redirects

  • 301 Moved Permanently
  • 302 Found
  • 303 See Other
  • 307 Temporary Redirect
  • 308 Permanent Redirect (Experimental)
  • Learn and love those HTTP 3xx redirects.
  • Use as many as necessary.
  • Careful with POST URLs: 301, 302, 303 switch to GET.
  • 307 and 308 force re-POST.

Drop the .php

  • Bad: http://example.com/about.php
  • Good: http://example.com/about
  • Don't expose the implementation.
  • MediaWiki/WordPress: don't put "mediawiki" or "wp" in URLs.

Pay Up

“Pretty much the only good reason for a document to disappear from the Web is that the company which owned the domain name went out of business or can no longer afford to keep the server running.”

Tim Berners-Lee, 1998
  • DNS is a weak link.
  • Don't let registration lapse. Pay for a decade.

Why Should I Care About URLs?

“We are building clans around languages we speak to the Machine.”

Vyacheslav Egorov, 2013
  • Clans around programming languages don't matter.
  • Languages, frameworks, best practices, platforms, operating systems come and go.
  • Only constant is the web itself.
  • Smartest thing: bet on the web.
  • Embrace things like URLS that make the web what it is.
  • Key part of the web, will outlast your clans.
  • URLs are part of the native language of the web. If you're going to speak to the web, you have to understand and respect that.
